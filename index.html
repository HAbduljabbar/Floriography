Floriography

The practice of using flowers to communicate coded messages, known as Floriography or the "language of flowers," is a fascinating historical and cultural phenomenon. While the symbolism of flowers dates back thousands of years across various cultures, its most popular and codified form emerged during the Victorian era (1837â€“1901) in Great Britain and the United States. In this intricate communication system, the color of the flower, along with its species, arrangement, and even presentation, played a crucial role in conveying specific sentiments and emotions that social etiquette often forbade speaking aloud.

The Role of Color in Floriography

In floriography, the color of a flower is a primary component of its symbolic meaning, often modifying or intensifying the message of the bloom itself. Different colors developed widely accepted, though not entirely universal, interpretations:

In this article we will illustrate the use of only three color of flowers which are:

  1. Red Flower
  2. White Flower
  3. Yellow Flower

Red Flower

The red flower usually used to represent:

  • Deep love,
  • passion,
  • desire,
  • courage,
  • respect.

White Flower

The white flower usually used to represent:

  • Purity,
  • innocence,
    • humility, reverence, sympathy, modesty,
  • new beginnings.

yalow Flower

The yellow flower usually used to represent:

  • Friendship,
  • joy,
  • happiness,
  • new beginnings. (Historically: jealousy or decrease of love, depending on the flower).

Therefore by carefully selecting a variety of colored flowers, an individual could construct a complex "talking bouquet" to communicate a detailed message without speaking a single word.
